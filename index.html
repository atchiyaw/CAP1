Governing Policies

Official policy and guidelines for Capstone Project and Research 1.

Covering requirements, schedule, defense procedures, manuscript structure, evaluation, and academic standards.

I Policy Statement

The Capstone Project and Research 1 shall serve as a culminating academic requirement designed to develop, assess, and demonstrate the students' ability to apply knowledge, skills, and competencies acquired in their program of study. It provides students an opportunity to identify real-world problems, propose technology-based or research-based solutions, conduct systematic inquiry, develop appropriate systems or outputs, and present their work through formal documentation and oral defense.

This policy establishes the official guidelines, schedule, group requirements, documentation format, defense procedures, expected outputs, and academic standards. All students enrolled shall comply with these guidelines to ensure uniformity, fairness, academic integrity, and quality.

The output must be relevant, feasible, ethical, technically sound, and beneficial to the target users, institution, community, or industry.

II Objectives of the Policy

This policy aims to:

  1. Provide a clear and organized framework for the conduct of Capstone Project and Research 1.
  2. Guide students in the preparation, documentation, development, and defense of their project.
  3. Establish uniform requirements for grouping, scheduling, documentation, and presentation.
  4. Ensure proposed projects are feasible, relevant, innovative, and aligned with program outcomes.
  5. Promote academic honesty, originality, collaboration, professionalism, and accountability.
  6. Define expected outputs and completion requirements for each stage of the process.
  7. Set standards for evaluating progress from concept development to final completion.

III Scope & Coverage

This policy applies to all students enrolled in Capstone Project and Research 1, covering the following activities:

Orientation Group Formation Conceptualization & Design Thinking Documentation Concept Defense Title Defense Progress Reports Final Defense System Development Compliance Reports

All students, group members, course teachers, advisers, and panel members shall be guided by this policy.

IV General Guidelines

A · Student Responsibility

Students are responsible for the planning, preparation, development, documentation, and presentation of their project. Work must be original, properly documented, ethically conducted, and submitted on time. Non-participation, repeated absences, or negligence may be subject to academic action.

B · Role of the Course Teacher

The course teacher supervises the overall conduct — providing orientation, monitoring group formation, guiding conceptualization, and determining feasibility. The teacher may require revisions, reject infeasible proposals, recommend regrouping, and enforce deadlines.

C · Academic Integrity

All outputs must be original. Plagiarism, fabrication of data, misrepresentation, and unauthorized use of existing systems are strictly prohibited. All sources must be properly cited. Violations are subject to institutional sanctions.

V Grouping Guidelines

Groups shall be composed of a minimum of three and a maximum of four members. Each group assigns internal roles, though all members remain collectively responsible for quality and completion.

VI Schedule of Activities

The following schedule guides the conduct of the course:

  1. June 3

    Orientation of the Capstone Project

    Discussion of the nature, objectives, requirements, expected outputs, grading, documentation format, defense procedures, and timeline.

  2. June 3–5

    Determination of Group Members

    Students form groups (3–4 members) and submit the final list to the course teacher for approval.

  3. June 8–12

    Conceptualization & Documentation

    Design thinking to identify problems, users, needs, and solutions. The required documentation format is presented and discussed.

  4. June 15–19

    Concept Defense

    Groups present proposed concepts. The teacher determines feasibility, relevance, originality, and technical soundness.

  5. June 22–26

    Title Defense

    Evaluation of the proposed title and the initial manuscript covering Chapters 1 to 3.

  6. June 29–July 3

    Submission of Required Documents

    Groups submit required documents per the prescribed format. Late or incomplete submissions may incur penalties.

VII–VIII Stages & Requirements of Defense

The course follows a progressive defense structure. Each stage must be completed before proceeding, unless permitted by the course teacher.

1

Concept Defense

  • Project concept
  • Problem setting
  • Proposed solution
  • Technical background

Determines if the project is relevant, feasible, and technically appropriate.

2

Title Defense

  • Chapter 1 — Introduction
  • Chapter 2 — Review of Related Literature & System
  • Chapter 3 — Technical Background

Establishes title, problem, objectives, scope, literature, and technical requirements.

3

Progress Report 1

  • Updated Chapters 1–3
  • Chapter 4 — Methodology
  • 60% working system

Core features, initial interface, database, and basic operational flow.

4

Progress Report 2

  • Updated Chapters 1–4
  • Chapter 5 — Results & Discussions
  • 90% working system

Nearly complete; most features functional, tested, and aligned with objectives.

5

Final Defense

  • Complete softbound manuscript
  • 100% working system
  • Compliance report
  • Final presentation & demo

May be accepted, accepted with revisions, or required for re-defense.

IX Required Manuscript Sections

The manuscript follows the prescribed chapter structure below. Click each chapter to expand.

  • A. Project Context — background of the study; the situation, issue, or opportunity that led to the project.
  • B. Purpose & Description — purpose and a clear description of the proposed system or output.
  • C. Objectives — general and specific objectives; clear, measurable, attainable, relevant.
  • D. Scope & Limitation — coverage, boundaries, and constraints of the project.
  • E. Definition of Terms — important terms defined operationally.
  • A. Related Literature — published sources supporting the project.
  • B. Related System — existing systems with features, strengths, and weaknesses.
  • C. Synthesis of the State-of-the-Art — trends, common features, and best practices.
  • D. Gap Bridged by the Study — the specific gap the project addresses.
  • A. Software Requirements — tools, languages, frameworks, databases, and libraries.
  • B. Hardware Requirements — devices, equipment, and infrastructure required.
  • A. Sources of Data — respondents, documents, interviews, surveys, logs, datasets.
  • B. Data Instrumentation — questionnaires, interview guides, checklists, rubrics, plus the development approach or process model.

Presents results of development, testing, evaluation, and implementation — including system features, testing results, user evaluation, and alignment with objectives. Tables, figures, and screenshots may be included.

  • A. Conclusions — directly answer the objectives, supported by findings.
  • B. Recommendations — realistic suggestions for improvement and future work.

X–XII Documentation, System & Compliance

X · Documentation Guidelines

Manuscripts follow the prescribed format with consistent grammar, citation style, pagination, headings, tables, and references. Formal academic language is required. All figures and appendices must be labeled and discussed. Required revisions must be incorporated before the next deadline.

XI · System Development

The system must align with the approved title, objectives, scope, and methodology, and develop progressively: 60% (PR1), 90% (PR2), 100% (Final). Evaluated on functionality, usability, reliability, completeness, and technical quality.

XII · Compliance Report

Required at Final Defense to document comments and how each was addressed. Columns: comment/recommendation, source, action taken, page or module affected, and status of compliance.

XIII–XV Attendance, Submission & Monitoring

XIII · Attendance & Participation

All members must attend orientations, consultations, and defenses. Absences are excused only for valid, approved reasons. Any member may be asked to explain the project during defense.

XIV · Submission Policy

Documents must be submitted on or before deadlines, following the required format, file type, and naming convention. Late or incomplete submissions may be deducted or rejected.

XV · Consultation & Monitoring

Groups are encouraged to consult regularly. The teacher may require progress checks, draft submissions, demos, or consultation logs. Failure to show progress may affect eligibility to proceed.

XVI Evaluation Guidelines

Evaluation may include the following criteria:

Relevance & significance of the problem Clarity of objectives Feasibility of the solution Quality of documentation Adequacy of literature review Soundness of technical background Appropriateness of methodology Progress & completeness of system Quality of presentation & defense Ability to answer questions Originality & academic integrity Compliance with recommendations

XVII Grounds for Revision, Rejection, or Re-Defense

A group may be required to revise, change, or defend again under any of the following:

  1. The project is not feasible within the given time or resources.
  2. The project does not address a clear problem.
  3. The title and objectives are not aligned.
  4. The manuscript is incomplete or poorly prepared.
  5. The system output does not match the approved proposal.
  6. The completion percentage does not meet the required level.
  7. The group fails to comply with previous comments.
  8. The project contains plagiarized or fabricated content.
  9. The group fails to demonstrate sufficient understanding.
  10. Major technical or documentation issues remain unresolved.

XVIII–XXI Ethics, Final Output & Effectivity

XVIII · Ethical Considerations

Projects involving users, personal information, or sensitive data must observe ethical standards — confidentiality, informed consent, and proper data handling. Systems with accounts or records must include security and privacy considerations.

XIX · Final Output Requirements

Each group submits: approved title, complete manuscript, functional system, required documentation, compliance report, presentation materials, and other documents required by the teacher.

XX–XXI · Effectivity & Closing

The policy takes effect upon approval and remains in force for the duration of the course. Capstone is a major undertaking requiring discipline, teamwork, creativity, technical competence, and professionalism.
